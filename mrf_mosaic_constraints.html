1. Why MRF: what Leiden and Ward both missed

Both Leiden and Ward attempted to enforce the mosaic constraint during clustering — by modifying edge weights or merge costs before committing to a partition. Both failed on the anisotropic benchmark (ARI stuck at 0.931 regardless of penalty strength). The root cause was the spatial pivot problem: penalizing the nearest spatially-close wrong-type candidate does not prevent merging with any of the many other wrong-type cells that are spatially distant and therefore unpenalized.

The wrong merges happen with spatially distant cells. Any penalty that only fires for spatially close pairs cannot box out a wrong cluster that is "everywhere in feature space."
The key shift: act on the final assignment, not on the merge path Instead of penalizing individual merge decisions, operate on the complete label assignment y = (y_1, ..., y_N) and penalize configurations where two spatially adjacent cells share the same label. This is evaluated globally, after all clustering decisions are made, and can inspect the full spatial neighborhood at once.

The MRF approach bypasses the routing problem entirely: it does not matter that the wrong merge happened via a spatially distant cell. What matters is the final assignment — and if cell i ends up same-labeled as one of its spatial neighbors, that is a direct, detectable violation.

Additionally, the Ward empirical analysis revealed that the spatial neighborhood composition is informative for 3 of the 5 misclassified cells in anisotropic (plurality-correct spatial neighbors), even though the feature neighborhood is majority wrong. An MRF can exploit this: the pairwise spatial term directly encodes "your spatial neighbors constrain what you can be."

2. The energy formulation

Treat cell type assignment as MAP inference in a pairwise MRF defined over the spatial graph. The energy to minimize is:

E(y) = Σ_i ψ_i(y_i) + λ · Σ_{(i,j) ∈ S} w_ij · φ(y_i, y_j) y_i ∈ {0, 1, ..., K-1} cell type assignment ψ_i(y_i) unary cost: how well does X_i fit cluster y_i? φ(y_i, y_j) pairwise cost: do cells i and j have conflicting types? S spatial edge set: pairs within spatial_radius w_ij ∈ (0, 1] proximity weight for spatial pair (i, j) λ global penalty strength

Lower E is better. The unary term comes from the initial feature clustering; the pairwise term encodes the mosaic prior. Finding the exact minimum of E is NP-hard in general for discrete MRFs with more than 2 labels, but several practical approximations work well for small K and sparse graphs.

3. Unary term: feature posteriors

The unary cost ψ_i(y_i) quantifies how well cell i's feature vector X_i fits cluster y_i. The natural choice when using GMM as the initial clusterer:

ψ_i(k) = -log P(y_i = k | X_i) = -log [ π_k · N(X_i; μ_k, Σ_k) / Σ_{k'} π_{k'} · N(X_i; μ_{k'}, Σ_{k'}) ]

where π_k, μ_k, Σ_k are the GMM mixture weight, mean, and covariance for component k. This posterior probability is the natural measure of ambiguity: cells with high max-posterior P(y_i = y* | X_i) ≈ 1 are unambiguous; cells with spread posteriors (max ≈ 0.4–0.6 for K=3) are candidates for spatial correction.

3a. Confidence as a gating mechanism

Before running MRF inference, identify ambiguous cells: those where the second-best posterior is non-negligible:

ambiguous(i) iff P(y_i = y*_i | X_i) < conf_threshold (e.g. 0.90) or P(y_i = 2nd best | X_i) > gap_threshold (e.g. 0.10)

Only ambiguous cells are candidates for reassignment. High-confidence cells are frozen — their labels act as anchors that stabilize the MRF. This prevents the pairwise term from accidentally flipping cells that the feature model has already correctly assigned.

Freezing high-confidence cells limits the damage radius The vast majority of cells (~95%) are unambiguous. Freezing them means the MRF only adjusts a small fraction. Incorrect reassignments are therefore bounded — the MRF cannot globally destabilize an already-good GMM solution.

3b. Ward posteriors

Ward does not naturally produce posterior probabilities. Three options for obtaining unary costs from Ward:

  • Distance to centroid: ψ_i(k) = ‖X_i − μ_k‖². Simple but ignores cluster shape.
  • Soft Ward: run Ward to get K centroids, then fit a GMM initialized at those centroids to get calibrated posteriors.
  • Mahalanobis distance: ψ_i(k) = (X_i − μ_k)ᵀ Σ_k⁻¹ (X_i − μ_k) using per-cluster covariance estimated from Ward members. This captures anisotropic clusters correctly.

The Mahalanobis option is the most principled. For anisotropic Gaussian clusters, it recovers the same posterior as GMM with equal priors and matching covariances.

4. Pairwise term: spatial conflict penalty

The pairwise term φ(y_i, y_j) encodes the mosaic constraint: two spatially adjacent cells should have different types.

4a. The Potts model

The simplest choice is the Potts penalty:

φ_Potts(y_i, y_j) = [y_i == y_j] (1 if same type, 0 otherwise)

Combined with proximity weights w_ij, the full pairwise cost is w_ij · [y_i == y_j]. This is the discrete mosaic constraint: any two adjacent cells sharing a type incur cost proportional to their proximity. The Potts model is the standard formulation for label smoothing in image segmentation — here it is used for the opposite purpose: label diversification rather than smoothing.

4b. Asymmetric variant: penalize only strong conflicts

Not all same-type spatial neighbor pairs are equally wrong. Two cells from the same mosaic type that happen to be nearby are a natural part of the mosaic structure (the type tiles the space — adjacent same-type cells are expected). The penalty should fire strongly only when proximity is high enough to suggest territorial overlap rather than same-tile adjacency.

phi_ramp(y_i, y_j, w_ij) = [y_i == y_j] · ramp(w_ij) ramp(w) = 0 for w <= tau_low = (w - tau_low)/(tau_high - tau_low) for tau_low < w < tau_high = 1 for w >= tau_high

This is the same dead-zone ramp from leiden_mosaic_constraints.html and ward_mosaic_constraints.html, now applied to the MRF pairwise term rather than to graph edge weights or merge costs.

4c. Type-asymmetric penalties

If prior knowledge about same-type contact rates is available (e.g., BC cells of the same type can have ~10% coverage fraction), the penalty can be made asymmetric:

phi_asym(y_i, y_j, w_ij) = [y_i == y_j] · max(0, w_ij - tau_low(y_i))

where tau_low(k) is the type-specific contact rate for type k. This is a direct generalization of the τ_low concept to the MRF setting.

5. Inference strategies

Minimizing E(y) exactly is NP-hard for K ≥ 3 labels. Three practical strategies exist, in increasing order of complexity:

Strategy Quality Speed Converges to
Greedy conflict resolution (V1) Weak O(N · deg) Local optimum
Iterated Conditional Modes (V2) Good O(N · K · deg · iters) Local optimum
Loopy Belief Propagation (V3) Better O(N · K² · deg · iters) Approximate marginals

For K=3 and N≈200 with sparse spatial graphs (~10 neighbors per cell), all three are fast enough to run interactively. The choice comes down to quality vs. implementation effort.

6. Greedy conflict resolution (V1)

The simplest approach: directly find and fix same-type spatial neighbor pairs.

Initial labels + posteriors

Run GMM (or Ward + Mahalanobis) to get labels y and per-cell posteriors P(y_i = k | X_i) for all k.

Collect violations

Find all spatial pairs (i, j) with w_ij > tau_low AND y_i == y_j. Sort by w_ij descending (closest conflicts first).

Resolve highest-weight conflict

For the top pair (i, j): pick the cell with lower max-posterior confidence (the "less certain" one). Compute the energy change dE for reassigning it to each alternative label k' != y_i. Reassign to the k' that gives the largest energy decrease. If no reassignment decreases E, skip this pair.

Update and recheck

After reassignment, recheck all spatial neighbors of the moved cell — new violations may have appeared. Re-sort the violation list. Repeat from step 2 until the list is empty or no energy-reducing move exists.

6a. Energy change formula for a single reassignment

When considering reassigning cell i from y_i to k':

dE(i, k') = [ψ_i(k') - ψ_i(y_i)] (unary change) + λ · Σ_{j ∈ spatial_nbrs(i)} w_ij · [φ(k', y_j) - φ(y_i, y_j)] (pairwise change)

Accept the move if dE(i, k') < 0. This is a local greedy descent — equivalent to one step of ICM (Section 7).

Greedy order matters and can trap the algorithm. If conflicts are resolved in the wrong order, resolving one may prevent a better global solution. Example: cells A, B, C form a spatial triangle, all assigned type 0. The correct solution is A=0, B=1, C=2. Greedily resolving A-B first may reassign B to type 1, then resolving B-C may reassign C to type 0, creating a new A-C conflict. The algorithm cycles or terminates at a suboptimal assignment.

6b. Mitigation: randomized restarts

Run greedy conflict resolution from multiple random orderings of violations. Keep the solution with lowest total E(y). For K=3 and sparse graphs, 10–20 restarts is typically sufficient to escape bad local optima.

7. Iterated Conditional Modes (V2)

ICM is the standard greedy MAP inference algorithm for MRFs. Instead of resolving conflicts one at a time, it sweeps over all cells repeatedly and updates each to its locally optimal label:

Initialize

Start from GMM/Ward labels y. Freeze high-confidence cells (posterior > threshold).

Cell sweep

For each unfrozen cell i (in any order), compute the local energy for each label k:

E_local(i, k) = ψ_i(k) + λ · Σ_{j ∈ spatial_nbrs(i)} w_ij · φ(k, y_j)

Assign y_i = argmin_k E_local(i, k). This minimizes the energy of cell i given its current neighbors' labels — "local MAP" for cell i.

Repeat sweeps

Continue sweeping until no cell changes label in a full sweep (convergence). Guaranteed to converge because total E(y) decreases monotonically with each accepted update.

7a. Key property: monotone convergence

Each ICM update either decreases E or leaves it unchanged. Therefore ICM always converges in finite steps. However, it converges to a local minimum — the quality depends strongly on initialization. Starting from a good GMM solution with only a few wrong labels, ICM should find the correct assignment quickly (typically 2–5 sweeps).

7b. ICM vs greedy conflict resolution

ICM considers all labels for every cell at every sweep, not just the current-label cells with violations. This means it can spontaneously correct cells that currently have no same-type spatial conflict but would benefit from reassignment in the context of other cells' labels. Greedy conflict resolution is a special case: it only updates cells involved in current violations.

ICM is strictly better than V1 with almost no added complexity ICM requires one extra loop over labels K at each cell update, and iterates until convergence instead of iterating over violations. For K=3 and N=200, the overhead is negligible. The quality improvement comes from considering all cells, not just violating ones.

8. Belief Propagation (V3)

Loopy belief propagation (LBP) is a message-passing algorithm that propagates probability distributions through the spatial graph rather than hard label assignments. It produces approximate marginal posteriors P(y_i = k | X, neighbors) that incorporate both the feature signal and the spatial context.

8a. Message passing

Each cell i sends a message m_{i→j}(k) to each spatial neighbor j representing how strongly cell i "believes" cell j should take label k:

m_{i→j}(k) ∝ Σ_{k'} exp(-ψ_i(k')) · exp(-λ · w_ij · φ(k', k)) · Π_{l ∈ nbrs(i)\{j}} m_{l→i}(k')

Messages are initialized uniformly and updated iteratively (min-sum or sum-product variant). After convergence, the belief at cell i is:

b_i(k) ∝ exp(-ψ_i(k)) · Π_{j ∈ nbrs(i)} m_{j→i}(k)

The MAP assignment is y_i = argmax_k b_i(k).

8b. When LBP is worth the complexity

LBP is overkill for the anisotropic benchmark (K=3, N=200, sparse graph). It becomes valuable when:

  • K is large (many cell types) and spatial context propagates through long chains
  • The spatial graph has long-range correlations that ICM's local updates miss
  • You need calibrated posterior probabilities, not just MAP assignments

For the toy_mosaics use case (K ≤ 6, N ≤ 300, local graph), ICM with good initialization is likely sufficient. LBP is the upgrade path if ICM plateaus.

9. Choosing lambda: posterior calibration

Lambda controls the trade-off between feature fidelity and spatial consistency. Unlike the Ward penalty (which needed to be calibrated in feature-variance units), the MRF lambda is more naturally calibrated because both terms are in energy units (negative log-probability).

9a. Natural scale: matching log-posterior magnitudes

For a GMM-based unary term, the typical magnitude of ψ_i(y_i) for a well-assigned cell is:

ψ_i(y*) = -log P(y_i = y* | X_i) ≈ 0 for confident cells (P ≈ 1) ≈ log K for maximally ambiguous cells (P ≈ 1/K)

For K=3, log K ≈ 1.1 nats. The pairwise term w_ij · φ = w_ij ≤ 1. So lambda ≈ 1 makes the pairwise penalty for a maximally close same-type pair (w=1, phi=1) comparable to a log-posterior gap of 1 nat — i.e., comparable to a 2.7× likelihood ratio. A reasonable default.

9b. Adaptive lambda: scale to confidence gap

The pairwise term should be large enough to flip ambiguous cells (small posterior gap) but not confident ones (large gap). The natural calibration:

lambda = alpha · median_posterior_gap posterior_gap(i) = P(y_i = 1st | X_i) - P(y_i = 2nd | X_i) median = over all cells (or over ambiguous cells only)

With alpha = 1 and a typical gap of ~0.6 for a 3-class GMM, lambda ≈ 0.6. A pairwise penalty of 1 (maximum proximity) then equals 1.67× the median gap — enough to flip a marginally ambiguous cell but not a confident one.

9c. The too-large lambda failure mode

If lambda is too large, the pairwise term dominates and the algorithm will try to make every spatial neighbor pair have different labels — producing a spatial coloring rather than a feature-based clustering. This is the opposite pathology: the spatial constraint overrides correct feature assignments.

Spatial coloring is not the mosaic constraint. The mosaic constraint says same-type cells should not be spatial neighbors. It does NOT say all spatial neighbor pairs must have different types — that would require K >= chromatic number of the spatial graph (typically 4-6 for planar graphs). For K=3 in a dense mosaic, some same-type adjacent pairs are inevitable and correct. Lambda must be small enough to allow this.

10. Ceiling analysis: what is actually fixable?

From the Ward empirical analysis, the 5 misclassified cells on anisotropic split into two categories. The MRF can only address one:

10a. Type A errors: fixable (cells 76, 77, 94)

These cells are correctly placed in the spatial mosaic (their mosaic tile), but have feature vectors that look like the wrong type. After GMM, they are assigned to the wrong cluster. However, they DO have same-type spatial conflicts: after GMM assigns them to cluster 2, they become same-type spatial neighbors with other cluster-2 cells that are in their spatial neighborhood.

Cell 76 (GMM: type 2, true: type 0): Spatial neighbors: 4×type0, 3×type1, 2×type2 (after GMM relabeling) After wrong assignment: cell 76 (type2) + 2 spatial nbrs (type2) = 3 same-type cells nearby → Same-type spatial conflict EXISTS → MRF can detect and reassign

The MRF ICM step for cell 76 would compute E_local(76, k) for k=0,1,2:

  • k=2 (current): ψ_76(2) [low, feature fits] + lambda · w · [76's type2 nbrs are same type] [high conflict]
  • k=0 (correct): ψ_76(0) [slightly higher, feature is less good] + lambda · w · [76's type0 nbrs are same type] [different conflict count]

If lambda is large enough, k=0 wins because assigning type0 resolves the type2 spatial conflicts without creating as many new type0 conflicts (since type0 cells are already present — they're the mosaic tile, not violations).

This reasoning is circular without ground truth. "Type0 nbrs are the mosaic tile" is only known post-correction. Before correction, we don't know which same-type neighbors are "correct tile" and which are "wrong assignment." ICM will update based on the current noisy labels. Whether it converges to the right solution depends on whether the initial GMM labels are good enough to provide a reliable context.

10b. Type B errors: not fixable (cells 176, 187)

These cells have no same-type spatial conflicts after any reasonable initial labeling, because their spatial neighborhood contains zero true-type-2 cells. There is no conflict to trigger MRF correction. Their only hope is a global restructuring of the partition — but the MRF, operating locally, cannot infer "there should be a type-2 cell here" without evidence from neighboring cells.

Maximum achievable ARI improvement from MRF If MRF correctly fixes all 3 Type-A errors and leaves 2 Type-B errors: ARI would improve from 0.931 to approximately 0.963 (rough estimate — exact value depends on cluster sizes). The 2 Type-B errors represent an irreducible floor for any spatially-local method given the feature distributions in anisotropic.yaml.

10c. A necessary condition for MRF correction to work

For MRF to correct a wrong assignment y_i = wrong, cell i must:

  1. Have at least one same-label spatial neighbor after the initial (wrong) assignment, OR have a spatial neighbor assigned to the correct label that would create a "pull" toward the correct label.
  2. Have a posterior gap small enough that the spatial term (lambda × pairwise) can overcome the unary preference for the wrong label.
  3. NOT be surrounded by other wrongly assigned cells that would reinforce the wrong label (echo chamber effect).

Cells 76, 77, 94 satisfy conditions 1 and 2. Whether condition 3 holds depends on how many of their spatial neighbors are themselves correctly assigned. From the data: 76 has 4 correct-type (type0) spatial neighbors out of 9 — a narrow plurality, but hopefully enough to provide a consistent pull toward the correct label.

11. Open questions

Does the MRF correction actually improve ARI for cells 76/77/94? The analysis shows the conditions are met in principle. The empirical test is whether ICM starting from GMM labels converges to the correct assignment for these cells, given the noisy neighborhood context.
What is the right lambda? The posterior-gap calibration (Section 9b) gives a principled starting point, but the actual threshold may be sensitive to the cluster geometry. Is there a way to set lambda from the data without cross-validation?
How many ICM sweeps are needed? For nearly-correct GMM initial labels, convergence should be fast (2-5 sweeps). But if the initial labels have more errors than anisotropic, the echo-chamber effect (condition 3 failure) could trap ICM in a wrong local minimum indefinitely.
Should the spatial graph use proximity scores or coverage fractions as weights w_ij? The Ward analysis showed both fail similarly as penalty signals, but for MRF the weights control the magnitude of the spatial force — a higher weight means a stronger pull toward label diversity. Proximity is simpler and more general; coverage fraction is more biologically principled.
What happens at the boundary of ARI improvement? If MRF correctly fixes cells 76/77/94 but introduces new errors elsewhere (false positives — correct cells that get flipped by an overly strong spatial force), the net ARI might not improve. What is the precision/recall of the MRF correction?
Can the MRF be used in an unsupervised way for real data (no ground truth)? The mosaic violation rate (fraction of same-type spatial neighbor pairs) could serve as a surrogate objective. Minimize violations while constraining feature compactness. This is directly observable without labels.
Is ICM sufficient, or do we need LBP? For the toy datasets (K=3, N≤300, sparse spatial graph), ICM should be equivalent to LBP in practice. The difference matters when the spatial graph has long chains or cycles where belief must propagate more than 1 hop. Worth benchmarking both on the test configs.

12. Visualization: mosaic loss vs feature similarity

The MRF is still not perfect after ICM. To understand why, we need a visualization that simultaneously shows how well a cell fits its assigned cluster in feature space and how much spatial conflict it incurs. These two axes define the four regimes of cell behavior — and only one regime is fixable by ICM.

12a. The core diagnostic: conflict–confidence scatter

Plot one point per cell. Horizontal axis = feature confidence; vertical axis = spatial conflict intensity. Both are computable from the fitted MRF without ground truth.

x_i = max_k P(y_i = k | X_i) [GMM max-posterior; high = confident] y_i = Σ_{j ∈ nbrs(i)} w_ij · [labels[j] == labels[i]] [total pairwise weight with same-type nbrs] Color: correct assignment (green) vs error (red), using ground-truth y if available. Shape: frozen cell (square) vs unfrozen (circle).

The four quadrants correspond to four fundamentally different situations:

Quadrant High confidence Low confidence
Low conflict Safe: frozen, correct, no pressure. The bulk of all cells. Spatially isolated ambiguous cell. ICM has no spatial signal to correct it. Type-B error.
High conflict Frozen + violated: ICM is blocked. Either the conflict is a real error frozen by overconfidence, or a correct cell whose neighbors are the problem. The MRF sweet spot: ambiguous AND conflicted. ICM should reassign these. Type-A error.
Diagnostic power of the scatter Any red point (error) in the top-left quadrant (frozen + violated) reveals cells where the confidence threshold is too high — the frozen cell is wrong but ICM cannot touch it. Lowering conf_threshold would expose it for reassignment. Any red point in the bottom-left (confident + no conflict) is a Type-B error: irreducible by any local spatial method.

12b. Energy gap map: spatial layout colored by ICM pressure

For each unfrozen cell, compute the energy gap between the two best labels:

gap_i = E_local(i, 2nd_best_k) - E_local(i, best_k)

Draw each cell's polygon in the spatial layout, colored by gap_i using a diverging colormap. Large positive gap = ICM is very confident in the current label (stable assignment). Gap near zero = ICM is about to flip (unstable, worth watching). Overlay misclassified cells with a bold outline. This map shows where in physical space the algorithm is uncertain, and whether uncertainty clusters near mosaic boundaries — which is expected and healthy — or in the interior of tiles, which would suggest the spatial term is overriding good feature assignments.

12c. Neighborhood composition bar chart for each error cell

For every cell that ends up misclassified after ICM, draw a horizontal stacked bar showing the type composition of its spatial neighbors before and after ICM (two bars per cell). Color segments by cluster type. Add a vertical dashed line at the cell's own type. This answers: "did the wrong-type neighbors reinforce the error, or did the correct neighbors fail to provide a sufficient pull?"

Example for cell 76 (true type 0, assigned type 2 after GMM): Before ICM: nbrs = [type0 ×4, type1 ×3, type2 ×2] → type0 plurality, type2 minority After ICM: nbrs = [type0 ×4, type1 ×3, type2 ×2] → (neighbors unchanged if they're frozen) The bar chart immediately reveals whether cell 76's type0 neighbors are numerous enough to provide a strong pull. If type2 neighbors reassign to type0 during ICM, the pull strengthens — visible as the type2 bar shrinking in the "after" bar.

12d. Feature-space decision boundaries with spatial conflict overlay

In 2D feature space (the standard blob scatter), draw the GMM decision boundaries as contours. Color each point by its spatial conflict score y_i (see 12a) rather than by label. High-conflict cells near the decision boundary are the ones ICM can help. High-conflict cells far from the boundary are likely misassigned neighbors pulling in the wrong direction — a sign that lam is too high or that the initial GMM was worse than expected.

Add a second panel showing the same plot colored by the energy gap (12b). Superimposing both panels reveals whether conflict and instability co-localize — they should, at a well-calibrated lam.

12e. ICM convergence trace per cell

For each cell that changes label during ICM (n_changed > 0), record the full label trajectory: (iteration, cell_i, old_label, new_label, E_before, E_after). Plot as a sequence of colored arrows in feature space: where the cell started, where it moved, and what the energy gain was. This shows whether ICM is correcting genuine errors or just shuffling ambiguous cells among equally-bad options (zero net energy change per flip).

Current limitation: the MRF model dict does not store per-cell trajectories. The fit() method only records n_changed (total) and violations_before/after (aggregate). To enable plots 12c–12e, the model dict needs to be extended with: per-cell label_before, label_after, and per-iteration n_changed_by_cell. This is a one-time change to mrf_mosaic.py with no algorithmic cost.

12f. Implementation sketch

All five visualizations should live in a new toy_mosaics/plot/mrf_diagnostics.py module, exporting a single entry point:

plot_mrf_diagnostics(dataset, result, *, ground_truth=None, axes=None) Subplots: [0] conflict–confidence scatter (12a) [1] spatial energy-gap map (12b) [2] neighborhood bar chart for misclassified cells (12c) — only if ground_truth provided [3] feature-space scatter + conflict overlay (12d) [4] ICM trajectory arrows in feature space (12e) — only if trajectory logged Returns (fig, axes) consistent with existing plot_mosaics / plot_blobs API.

The ground_truth argument is optional — without it, plots 0 and 2 are still useful as per-cell conflict diagnostics; they just cannot distinguish errors from correct assignments.

13. Test suite design for MRF across configs

The current test suite (tests/test_clustering.py) covers shape/dtype/reproducibility for KMeans, GMM, and Leiden but has no MRF-specific tests and does not include the hard configs (anisotropic, high_overlap, elongated_clusters). It also has no quality assertions — no ARI checks, no violation counts. Here is the full test suite design for MRF.

13a. Coverage gaps in the current suite

  • No MRF tests at all. MRFMosaicStrategy is not imported or referenced in the test file.
  • Config coverage is too easy. example/circles/moons all have well-separated, isotropic clusters — they cannot expose the ICM-correctability problem. anisotropic and elongated_clusters are the only configs where MRF has something to fix.
  • No regression assertions. Tests pass as long as the code runs without error. A refactor that silently regresses ARI from 0.95 to 0.70 would pass all tests.
  • No diagnostic output for failures. When a test fails on a specific cell, there is no printout of which cells were wrong, their posteriors, or their spatial neighborhoods. The failure message is just "ARI below threshold" with no actionable detail.
  • No before/after comparison. No test verifies that MRF improves over plain GMM — only that MRF produces valid output.

13b. Proposed test structure

Organize tests into three layers: smoke (shape/dtype/reproducibility), quality (ARI thresholds, violation counts), and diagnostic (per-cell detail on failures). Only the diagnostic layer uses parametrize-per-config; the smoke tests can stay on easy configs.

Smoke tests (existing pattern, extended to MRF)

Parameterize MRFMosaicStrategy alongside KMeans/GMM in the existing STRATEGIES list. Verify labels.shape, labels.dtype, n_unique_labels == n_mosaics, model dict keys present (gmm, lam, n_iters, n_frozen, n_changed, violations_before, violations_after). One config (example) is sufficient — these tests only check the API contract.

Quality tests: MRF improves over GMM baseline

For each hard config (anisotropic, elongated_clusters, high_overlap): run GMMStrategy and MRFMosaicStrategy on the same dataset, compute ARI against ground truth for both. Assert ARI(MRF) >= ARI(GMM) - 0.01 (non-regression: MRF must not hurt). For anisotropic specifically, assert ARI(MRF) >= 0.94 (weak lower bound above the known GMM baseline of 0.931). Use pytest.approx or direct comparison with a tolerance.

Quality test: violations reduce

Assert that result.model["violations_after"] <= result.model["violations_before"] for all configs. ICM is monotone, so this is guaranteed by the algorithm — but making it an explicit test catches any regression in the energy formulation (e.g. sign flip in lam) that would silently break monotonicity.

Quality test: frozen cells are never reassigned

Compare GMM initial labels (extracted from result.model["gmm"].predict(dataset.X)) against MRF final labels for high-confidence cells. Assert that no cell with GMM max-posterior >= conf_threshold changed label. This guards against a bug where the frozen mask is not applied correctly.

Diagnostic fixture: per-cell error summary

A pytest fixture or helper function error_summary(dataset, result) returns a list of dicts — one per misclassified cell — with fields: cell_id, true_type, assigned_type, gmm_max_posterior, spatial_conflict_score, n_same_type_neighbors, n_correct_type_neighbors, was_frozen. Print this summary with print() so it appears in pytest -s output even when the test passes. When a quality test fails, the summary should immediately tell you which cells are wrong and why.

13c. Diagnostic output format

The error summary should be emitted as a table (via tabulate or plain string formatting) on stdout. Example for anisotropic with a partially-correct MRF run:

The "→" interpretive lines are generated automatically based on the conflict score: if conflict == 0, the cell is labelled "Type-B (no spatial signal)"; if conflict > 0 and frozen, "Type-A frozen (lower conf_threshold to fix)"; if conflict > 0 and unfrozen, "Type-A unfixed (increase lam or check echo chamber)".

13d. Config-specific parametrization

Add anisotropic, elongated_clusters, and high_overlap to a separate parametrized fixture used only for quality + diagnostic tests, distinct from the easy configs used for smoke tests. This avoids slowing down the fast smoke test suite.

HARD_CONFIGS = ["anisotropic", "elongated_clusters", "high_overlap"] @pytest.fixture(params=HARD_CONFIGS) def hard_dataset(request): cfg = _load_cfg(request.param) return dataset_from_config(cfg), request.param # yield config name for diagnostics def test_mrf_ari_nonnegression(hard_dataset): dataset, name = hard_dataset gmm_result = GMMStrategy(n_clusters=dataset.n_mosaics).fit(dataset) mrf_result = MRFMosaicStrategy(n_clusters=dataset.n_mosaics).fit(dataset) ari_gmm = adjusted_rand_score(dataset.y, gmm_result.labels) ari_mrf = adjusted_rand_score(dataset.y, mrf_result.labels) summary = error_summary(dataset, mrf_result) print(f"\n[{name}] GMM ARI={ari_gmm:.3f} MRF ARI={ari_mrf:.3f}") print(summary) assert ari_mrf >= ari_gmm - 0.01, f"MRF regressed: {ari_mrf:.3f} < {ari_gmm:.3f}"

13e. Label-matching caveat

Both GMM and MRF return labels in arbitrary cluster order (cluster 0 may correspond to true type 2). ARI handles this automatically via the confusion matrix, but the per-cell diagnostic needs an explicit label mapping step before computing "true_type vs assigned_type". Use sklearn.utils.linear_assignment_ or scipy.optimize.linear_sum_assignment on the confusion matrix to find the best permutation before printing the summary table.

The permutation must be re-computed for each result independently. If GMM assigns cluster 0 → type 1 and MRF assigns cluster 0 → type 2 (due to a different initialization), comparing labels without remapping will produce nonsense error counts. Always remap to ground truth before reporting.

13f. What "good summaries focused on wrong cells" actually means

A test that just asserts ARI >= 0.94 gives you a pass/fail. A test with the diagnostic summary above tells you:

  • Exactly which cells are wrong (cell IDs, so you can find them in spatial maps)
  • Why they are wrong in the MRF framework (Type-A fixable, Type-A frozen, Type-B no-signal)
  • Whether the errors are new (introduced by MRF from a correct GMM assignment) or inherited (already wrong in GMM)
  • What parameter to tune to potentially fix each error type (conf_threshold for frozen Type-A; lam for unfixed Type-A; none for Type-B)

This makes test failures actionable rather than just informative.
