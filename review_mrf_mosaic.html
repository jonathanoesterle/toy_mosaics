Theoretical Review: mrf_mosaic.py

GMM + ICM spatial label refinement via coverage-fraction pairwise term

File: toy_mosaics/mrf_mosaic.py  ·  Reviewed: 2026-05-28  ·  Scope: algorithmic / theoretical (not style / performance)

Executive Summary

The module implements a Markov Random Field (MRF) approach to spatial cell-type assignment: a GMM provides initial per-cell label distributions (unary costs), and ICM iteratively minimises a pairwise energy that penalises same-label territorial overlap measured by polygon coverage fractions (CF). The algorithm is well-motivated and contains several theoretically sound design choices. The main limitations are those inherent to local optimisation (ICM is coordinate descent, not global), the circularity in using GMM confidence to calibrate the very spatial thresholds that govern ICM, and a collection of heuristic multipliers (lam_boost, lam_alpha, quantile choices) whose theoretical grounding is limited.

Strengths

Theoretical Strengths

14 items
S1 Log-likelihood unary avoids the zero-posterior problem energy

Unary cost is -(log p(X_i | cluster k) + log π_k) — the negative log-unnormalised posterior. This equals the full negative log-posterior up to a per-cell constant that cancels in argmin. Critically, it avoids the zero-posterior problem: far-from-mean cells still receive a finite (large) unary cost rather than -∞, keeping the ICM energy well-defined everywhere. Including log π_k ensures ICM at lam=0 exactly reproduces the GMM assignment, preventing spurious flips driven by mixing-weight asymmetries.

S2 Coverage-fraction pairwise term encodes the mosaic constraint directly algorithm

Using polygon coverage fractions as pairwise weights is a principled, domain-specific choice. CF measures how much two cells' convex hulls overlap; for a true mosaic (non-overlapping same-type tiling) same-type neighbours should have low CF. The ramp maps CF to a weight in [0, 1] that is zero within the expected same-type contact range and grows toward 1 for genuine territorial violations. This directly operationalises the biological prior without requiring hand-crafted spatial features.

S3 ICM guarantees monotone energy descent convergence

Iterated Conditional Modes (Gauss-Seidel variant) is guaranteed to monotonically decrease the MRF energy at each cell update, and therefore to converge to a local minimum in finite steps for a finite label set. The energy function used here — unary plus bilinear pairwise — is exactly the class of energies for which this guarantee holds.

S4 Tau calibration fallback hierarchy is robust calibration

The three-tier fallback (frozen same/diff pairs → all pairs → hard prior) handles data scarcity gracefully. The guard condition tau_low + min_gap < tau_high prevents the ramp from collapsing to a step function. Clamping to [0.01, 0.49] avoids degenerate thresholds. Each calibration decision is recorded in tau_calib, making the process fully auditable.

S5 Lambda calibration is derived from the energy scale calibration

Rather than asking the user to guess lam in arbitrary units, the auto-calibration normalises it by the median unary gap and median pairwise push. The target condition — "a cell at the median unary gap flips when it receives lam_alpha average-weight violating contacts" — is physically interpretable and dimensionally consistent with the energy function.

S6 Signed ramp encodes both attraction and repulsion energy

A purely repulsive pairwise term (plain ramp) cannot distinguish between "this pair has low CF because they are same-tile neighbors" and "this pair has low CF because they barely interact." The signed ramp makes the energy attractive for CF < τ_low, encoding the biological prior that low-CF same-type adjacency is evidence of shared territory, not a violation. This is theoretically richer and reduces false flips at tile boundaries.

S7 Log-ramp gives super-linear penalty for high-overlap violations energy

A linear ramp treats a 60 % territorial overlap as twice as bad as 30 %, but intuitively a 90 % overlap is far more than three times as bad as 30 %. The log-ramp log(1 + α·(CF − τ)) / log(1 + α·(1 − τ)) grows rapidly near CF = 1, providing strong corrective force for near-complete territorial conflicts while being tolerant of borderline overlaps.

S8 Conflict resolution treats symmetric pairs jointly algorithm

Single-cell ICM cannot resolve the case where two cells are in each other's optimal assignment cycle: flipping one is only beneficial conditional on the other also flipping. _resolve_conflicting_pairs explicitly solves each same-label repulsive pair as a joint 2-cell K×K sub-problem, finding the jointly optimal assignment. This is a direct fix for a well-known failure mode of coordinate descent on MRFs.

S9 H1 pre-pass prevents frozen-cell lock-in algorithm

Unfreezing both cells in a CF > θ_hard same-label pair (rather than just the less-confident one) correctly handles feature impostors: cells whose feature vectors fall inside the wrong cluster distribution may be frozen with higher GMM confidence than the correctly-assigned neighbour. Unfreezing both lets the spatial term dominate in ICM, which correctly sees the geometric impossibility.

S10 Post-merge unary pools GMM components via log-sum-exp algorithm

After merging sub-clusters, the unary for the merged cluster is computed as -logaddexp(-unary_c₁, -unary_c₂, …), which is exactly the negative log-likelihood of a mixture of the constituent GMM components. This is mathematically correct: cells from absorbed sub-clusters score properly under their original component rather than being forced to match the surviving representative's Gaussian.

S11 Residual violator feature guard prevents unsupported assignments data

In _label_residual_violators, the chosen label must pass a minimum posterior threshold computed via softmax over the unary costs. This prevents spatially driven reassignments for cells with no feature support for the target label, labelling them -1 instead. The combined spatial + feature criterion is more principled than spatial reassignment alone.

S12 Per-cluster τ_low adapts the dead zone to each cluster's CF baseline calibration

When per_cluster_tau=True (default), τ_low is calibrated independently for each cluster from that cluster's own frozen same-label CF distribution. Clusters with systematically higher intra-tile coverage fractions — e.g., large-polygon cell types or dense mosaics — receive a correspondingly higher dead zone so the ramp only fires for contacts that are anomalous relative to that cluster's own baseline, not the global average. Falls back to global τ_low for clusters with fewer than _MIN_CALIB_PAIRS frozen same-label pairs. Directly addresses the case where polygon sizes vary across cell types (see W6 / RWD6).

S13 EM re-fit loop re-anchors the GMM on spatially-corrected labels algorithm

After the main ICM (n_em_iters=1 by default), the GMM is re-fitted warm-started from the current cluster centroids. New Gaussian means and covariances are anchored on the spatially-corrected assignment, and another ICM pass runs on fully-unfrozen cells. This breaks the normalisation trap: a cell that is far from all cluster Gaussians can still receive a high posterior for the wrong label because all posteriors sum to 1 — the updated GMM shifts the Gaussian away from the impostor and toward the correct spatial territory. Running the post-EM ICM with no frozen cells ensures the new unary costs are not over-ridden by a stale confidence mask.

S14 Tracked cleanup loop with stochastic perturbations escapes cleanup fixed points convergence

When n_tracked_cleanup_iters > 0, the full alternating cleanup sequence (pairwise conflict resolution + residual violator assignment) is run N times, each time optionally perturbing the unary costs with Gaussian noise (cleanup_sigma) and temporarily unfreezing a random fraction of frozen cells (cleanup_unfreeze_frac). The iteration with the lowest total MRF energy is returned — oscillations between cleanup and EM passes cannot regress the output because the tracker keeps a copy of the best state seen. A fixed-point early stop avoids wasted iterations. The energy is always evaluated on the original, unperturbed unary so the winner selection is unbiased. This turns the deterministic cleanup into a lightweight stochastic search over local optima.

Weaknesses

Theoretical Weaknesses & Solutions

13 items
W1 ICM finds a local minimum — result depends on GMM initialisation convergence

ICM is coordinate descent on a non-convex discrete energy function. It is guaranteed to converge, but only to a local minimum whose quality depends heavily on the starting labels. If the GMM initialisation is far from the ground truth (e.g., a bad random seed, highly overlapping feature distributions), ICM may get trapped in a poor basin and produce worse results than the raw GMM. There is no mechanism to escape local minima (no simulated annealing, graph cuts, or restarts).

Solutions
Moderate ★ Recommended Multi-restart with energy selection. Run ICM from n independent GMM seeds (varying random_state) and keep the result with the lowest total MRF energy. Requires implementing global energy tracking (see W13) first; the restart loop itself is trivial. Cost scales linearly with n.
Hard Alpha-expansion / graph cuts. For submodular pairwise energies, alpha-expansion finds solutions within a known factor of the global optimum in polynomial time. Requires verifying that the pairwise term is submodular (it is, for non-negative weights and the indicator [k_i == k_j]), then integrating PyMaxflow or a similar max-flow library.
Hard Simulated annealing ICM. Accept energy-increasing moves with probability exp(−ΔE / T) and anneal T over sweeps. Escapes shallow local minima at the cost of many more sweeps and a temperature schedule that must be tuned per dataset.
Implementation
Done n_restarts parameter added. Coverage map built once and shared. Best result selected by energy_final (W13). Works as described — no complications.
W2 Tau calibration is circular: it uses GMM confidence to set thresholds that govern ICM calibration

Frozen cells are defined by GMM posterior ≥ conf_threshold and then used as ground-truth proxies to estimate τ_low, τ_high, and lam. These thresholds in turn govern which pairs ICM will correct. If the GMM is poorly calibrated (wrong means, overlapping components), the frozen cells will mislabel the same/diff-type CF distributions, biasing all three parameters in correlated ways. The algorithm has no independent check on GMM quality before using it as a calibration oracle.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended GMM quality gate before calibration. Before using frozen cells as a calibration oracle, compute a simple self-consistency score: the fraction of frozen cells whose nearest spatial neighbour (in the coverage map) has the same label. If this falls below a threshold (e.g. 0.6), emit a warning and fall back to the hard priors rather than propagating a bad GMM into tau. No new code path is needed — just an early-exit condition in _calibrate_tau.
Moderate ★ Recommended Spatial-only proxy for frozen cells. Use a purely spatial criterion to identify high-confidence cells, independent of the GMM: cells whose k-nearest spatial neighbours all have low pairwise CF (CF < some_prior_low) are likely same-tile members regardless of their feature confidence. These spatially-grounded cells replace GMM-frozen cells in the calibration step, breaking the circular dependency.
Moderate ★ Recommended Iterative recalibration. After ICM converges, re-estimate τ_low/τ_high using the ICM labels (which are generally better than the raw GMM) as the oracle, rebuild the pairwise weights, and run a second ICM pass. One iteration of this EM-like loop often corrects biases introduced by the initial circular estimate.
Implementation
Default off Quality gate implemented in _calibrate_tau (gmm_quality_gate=0.0 default). Enabling it changes the calibrated lam, which cascades: weaker ICM leaves frozen cells in violation, and conflict resolution then moves them — breaking the frozen-cells invariant test. Needs a joint fix with the lam convergence target before it can be safely enabled.
Done Iterative recalibration implemented (recalibrate=False default). Runs a second ICM pass after re-estimating tau from post-ICM labels. No complications.
Skipped Spatial-only proxy not implemented. The quality gate (once stabilised) subsumes much of its benefit.
W3 Gauss-Seidel and Jacobi ICM converge to different solutions algorithm

The sequential (n_workers=1) and parallel (n_workers≠1) code paths implement fundamentally different ICM variants. Gauss-Seidel uses immediately updated labels; Jacobi updates all cells simultaneously. Both converge, but they minimise slightly different implicit objectives and can reach different local minima on the same input. The documentation notes this but treats it as a minor practical detail, whereas it constitutes a reproducibility concern: identical inputs produce different results depending on n_workers.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended Decouple parallelism from ICM variant. Use the sparse-matrix Jacobi path only for the coverage-map build (which is embarrassingly parallel and order-independent), then always run Gauss-Seidel ICM regardless of n_workers. Documents the intent: n_workers controls build parallelism only, not the ICM update rule. Removes the reproducibility gap at zero algorithmic cost.
Moderate Randomised asynchronous Gauss-Seidel. Replace the fixed cell sweep order in sequential ICM with a random permutation each iteration (standard practice in MRF literature). This strictly improves convergence speed on average and makes the sequential path less sensitive to the specific sweep order, while remaining deterministic given a fixed random_state.
Implementation
Done icm_jacobi=False by default — ICM is always Gauss-Seidel regardless of n_workers. The Jacobi path is still available via icm_jacobi=True. n_workers now exclusively controls coverage-map parallelism. Removes the reproducibility gap at zero cost.
W4 GMM assumes Gaussian features — miscalibrated unary for non-Gaussian data data

The unary cost is derived from Gaussian component log-likelihoods. For multimodal, heavy-tailed, or skewed feature distributions, these costs will be wrong: the algorithm will assign high unary cost to cells that legitimately belong to the cluster but lie in a non-Gaussian tail, biasing ICM toward keeping them misassigned even when spatial evidence is strong. The full-covariance GMM also becomes increasingly unreliable in high dimensions (curse of dimensionality) due to the number of free covariance parameters.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended Expose covariance_type as a parameter. Adding covariance_type: str = "full" to MRFMosaicStrategy.__init__ lets users switch to "diag" or "tied" for high-dimensional data. Diagonal covariance has K·D free parameters instead of K·D², greatly reducing overfitting risk. Zero algorithmic change.
Moderate ★ Recommended Kernel density unary (non-parametric fallback). After GMM fitting, estimate the true per-cluster density with a kernel density estimator (KDE) on each cluster's cells. Replace log_liks with KDE log-densities. The GMM still initialises the labels and provides the cluster assignment; KDE only corrects the unary costs for non-Gaussian tails. Adds a dependency on scipy.stats.gaussian_kde.
Hard Learnable unary via a small neural density estimator. Train a normalizing flow or a deep ensemble per cluster (after GMM initialisation) to replace the Gaussian log-likelihood. Theoretically the best fix for arbitrary feature distributions, but requires a differentiable framework, per-cluster training loops, and careful handling of small cluster sizes.
Implementation
Done covariance_type="full" parameter added. No complications.
Done kde_unary=False parameter added. Falls back to GMM Gaussian when a cluster has fewer than 2 cells. No complications.
W5 Lambda calibration approximates the actual ramp with a linear ramp calibration

The push estimate in _calibrate_tau uses linear_ramp(CF) = (CF − τ_low) / (1 − τ_low) as a "conservative approximation" of the actual ramp, regardless of whether log_ramp=True or signed_ramp=True. For a log-ramp with α=10, the linear approximation significantly underestimates the actual pairwise force at high CF values, causing lam to be over-estimated and potentially over-correcting borderline conflicts.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended Pass the actual ramp_fn into _calibrate_tau. The ramp function is already constructed before calibration is called (it is a closure over log_ramp_alpha). Adding a ramp_fn parameter to _calibrate_tau and replacing the hardcoded linear formula with ramp_fn(cf, tau_low, tau_high) in the push loop is a one-line change that eliminates the approximation entirely.
Moderate Empirical lam calibration via simulation. Given τ_low, τ_high, and the actual ramp_fn, simulate the flip condition numerically: for a synthetic two-cell sub-problem at the median unary gap, binary-search for the lam that causes a flip with lam_alpha average-weight violating contacts. This makes the calibration exact for any ramp shape.
Implementation
Reverted Key insight: the actual ICM ramp _ramp(cf, τ_low, τ_high) saturates at 1 for CF > τ_high, while the linear push approximation uses 1 − τ_low as denominator (reaching 1 only at CF=1). Most repulsive contacts have CF > τ_high, so switching to the actual ramp inflates median_push by ~3×, deflating calibrated lam by the same factor. Weaker ICM leaves frozen cells in violation; conflict resolution then moves them by treating their inflated neighbourhood energy as a signal to switch labels. The fix requires jointly adjusting the calibration convergence target — not a one-line change.
W6 Tau quantile choices (p99 / p25) are not theoretically derived calibration

τ_low = p99 of same-label CF and τ_high = p25 of diff-label CF are reasonable rules of thumb but lack a principled derivation from the mosaic model. The asymmetry (p99 vs p25) implies the ramp is designed to tolerate almost all same-type contacts while penalising 75 % of cross-type ones, but this is not formally motivated. Different mosaic geometries (e.g., high-overlap configs) may require different quantile choices that the current defaults will not adapt to.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended Expose quantile levels as parameters. Add tau_low_q: float = 0.99 and tau_high_q: float = 0.25 to MRFMosaicStrategy and thread them into _calibrate_tau. This immediately lets users adapt to different overlap regimes without any theoretical commitment, and makes the current defaults explicitly visible and overridable.
Moderate ★ Recommended Fit a two-component mixture to the CF distribution. Model the observed CF values as a mixture of a same-type component (low CF, approximately Beta) and a diff-type component (higher CF). The EM-estimated crossover point gives a data-driven τ_low that adapts to the actual same-type CF range, replacing the fixed p99 heuristic. Requires only scipy.stats.
Hard Derive τ from the analytical Poisson-disk CF distribution. For a Poisson-disk mosaic with known exclusion radius and cell density, the expected intra-tile CF distribution can be approximated analytically. Setting τ_low at the p99 of this analytical distribution (parameterised by spatial_radius / mean_diameter) would give a principled, geometry-aware threshold. Requires deriving or approximating the CF distribution for the specific polygon-coverage-fraction geometry used.
Implementation
Done tau_low_q=0.99 and tau_high_q=0.25 parameters added and threaded into _calibrate_tau. No complications.
Done CF mixture model implemented (cf_tau_mixture=False default). Fits a 2-component GMM to all CF values. No complications as opt-in.
W7 Conflict resolution bypasses the frozen mask — can override confident GMM cells algorithm

_resolve_conflicting_pairs deliberately bypasses frozen, meaning it can re-assign cells that the GMM assigned with very high confidence. The justification (feature impostors) is correct in cases where the GMM is wrong, but the algorithm has no way to distinguish a genuine impostor from a legitimately high-confidence cell that happens to have a high-CF same-label neighbour due to noise or a borderline geometric configuration. In the latter case, the conflict resolution introduces an error.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended Feature-consistency guard on accepted swaps. After computing the joint-optimal assignment (k_i*, k_j*), only accept the swap if the GMM posterior of each cell for its new label exceeds a minimum threshold (e.g. min_posterior=0.01, already used in _label_residual_violators). This blocks swaps that are spatially motivated but have zero feature support, at the cost of potentially leaving some true impostors uncorrected.
Moderate Graduated bypass based on impostor score. Compute an impostor suspicion score for each frozen cell: the number of high-CF same-label repulsive contacts divided by total contacts. Only bypass the frozen mask for cells whose suspicion score exceeds a threshold (e.g., most neighbours are violations). This preserves the frozen status of cells that appear in at most one borderline conflict.
Implementation
Done Feature-consistency guard enabled by default (conflict_min_posterior=0.01). The very permissive threshold (≥1 % posterior) only blocks swaps with near-zero feature support, allowing all spatially-motivated moves that have any plausible feature explanation. At this threshold the order-dependence issue (noted in the earlier "Default off" entry) is negligible in practice: few swaps are blocked mid-round, so the cascading effect on frozen-cell neighbourhood energy is minimal. Safe to use without theta_hard.
W8 Boosted-lambda phase uses a heuristic multiplier with no theoretical basis heuristic

Phase 2 of _resolve_conflicting_pairs re-runs with lam_eff = lam × lam_boost (default 5×) for pairs with w ≥ lam_boost_min_w (default 0.7). The factor 5× and threshold 0.7 are not derived from the energy model. Amplifying lam by 5× near the boundary of the repulsive zone risks over-correcting cells whose large unary gap is legitimate (they genuinely belong to the initially-assigned type) rather than caused by feature impostor effects.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended Set lam_boost from the unary gap distribution. Instead of a fixed 5×, set lam_boost = p95_unary_gap / (lam × lam_boost_min_w), i.e. the boost needed so that lam_eff × w_ij exceeds the 95th percentile of unary gaps for a pair at threshold weight. This makes the boost adaptive: it is exactly strong enough to flip the hardest legitimate conflict, and no stronger.
Moderate Replace Phase 2 with a dedicated spatial-only pass. For pairs with w ≥ lam_boost_min_w that remain in conflict after Phase 1, ignore the unary entirely and assign labels purely to minimise the pairwise violation (i.e., set lam → ∞ in the 2-cell sub-problem). This is the correct limiting behaviour when spatial evidence is overwhelming, and avoids the arbitrary 5× scaling.
Implementation
Done Adaptive boost implemented with a separate adaptive_lam_boost=False flag. When enabled, computes p95_gap / (lam × 0.7) clamped to [5.0, 20.0] — the lower bound of 5.0 preserves original baseline behaviour. Key insight: an uncapped lower bound (tested at 2.0) caused the same cascade as W5-revert: weaker Phase 2 left violations for residual-violators to assign as −1. The 5.0 floor is load-bearing.
W9 Greedy merge order may be suboptimal — wrong merges are irreversible algorithm

_merge_clusters_to_k greedily merges the pair with smallest L2 feature-mean distance at each step. A wrong merge at step t is irreversible: the two mis-merged sub-clusters' cells are pooled into one cluster, and all subsequent ICM, EM, and cleanup passes must fight the resulting territorial violation. In practice, the pipeline reduces errors considerably but cannot fully overcome the wrong merge — a residual pocket of mis-assigned cells persists as long as the wrongly merged cluster exists. This is visible in figures as a "stub" of one colour sitting inside another type's territory even after the final cleanup.

The spatial veto (fraction of high-CF pairs) prevents obviously wrong merges, but uses noisy pre-merge labels for its estimate. The greedy order further compounds the problem: an early "cheap" merge can block the correct merge from ever becoming the next-best option.

Solutions
Easy Switch to average-linkage merge cost. Replace L2 distance between cluster means with the average pairwise L2 distance across all cells of the two clusters (i.e. Ward-like criterion). Average linkage is less prone to chaining than single-linkage (closest-mean), producing more compact merged clusters without requiring a global optimisation. Note: implemented then deferred — changing the merge order alters cluster membership mid-merge, causing subsequent spatial-gate checks to fail. Average linkage requires co-designing the gate structure so eligibility is evaluated on original (pre-merge) cell sets.
Moderate ★ Recommended Constrained global dendrogram — enumerate all valid merge trees upfront. Instead of a greedy sequence, build a full constrained dendrogram before any merge runs:
  1. Compute all pairwise feature-mean distances between K_eff sub-clusters: O(K_eff²).
  2. Mark each pair as valid (passes both spatial gates) or invalid (fails adjacency or fraction veto).
  3. Run Ward / average-linkage hierarchical clustering restricted to valid pairs only. If a pair is invalid it is assigned infinite distance — it can never be merged.
  4. Read off the cut of the dendrogram at K_target clusters.
Cost analysis: with K_eff ≤ 15 sub-clusters and n ≈ 200 cells, all pairwise sub-cluster feature distances cost O(K_eff² × (n/K_eff)²) ≈ 4 000 distance computations — negligible. The dendrogram itself is O(K_eff³) ≈ 3 375 operations. The spatial gate evaluation (already done for the greedy merge) is reused. Total overhead: effectively zero.

Key advantage: the merge decision is now globally optimal over all spatially-valid sequences, not just locally greedy. Wrong merges that were "cheap" in feature distance but blocked the correct merge are no longer taken.
Moderate ★ Recommended Post-pipeline merge correction — redo the merge after the full pipeline. The first merge uses pre-ICM labels that are noisy. After the full pipeline (merge → second ICM → EM → cleanup), the labels are far better. Use this improved state to detect and correct wrong merges:
  1. Detect suspicious merged clusters by at least one of: (a) high within-cluster CF violations surviving after cleanup; (b) spatial disconnectedness — the cluster's cells form ≥ 2 disconnected spatial components (same test used in _split_fragmented_clusters); (c) feature bimodality — a 2-component GMM on the cluster's cells is a significantly better fit than a 1-component fit.
  2. Re-split suspicious clusters at their natural spatial boundaries (using the existing DBSCAN / k-NN connectivity test on the cluster's cells only).
  3. Re-merge the new sub-clusters using the constrained global dendrogram (solution W9.2 above), now scored against the post-pipeline feature distribution rather than the noisy pre-ICM one.
  4. Run another cleanup pass to tidy up boundaries.
Why "later" is better: after EM, the GMM means are anchored on the spatially-corrected cell populations. Cells from the two wrongly-merged sub-clusters will have drifted apart in the GMM (each EM iteration shifts the mean toward the cells that are spatially consistent with that cluster), making the re-split signal cleaner. The spatial disconnectedness signal is also stronger after cleanup — the surviving error stub is usually spatially isolated from the bulk of the wrong cluster.
Moderate ★ Recommended Combined approach: global dendrogram + post-pipeline correction.
  1. Replace the greedy first merge with the constrained global dendrogram (W9.2). This eliminates most wrong merges at essentially zero extra cost.
  2. After the full pipeline, run the post-pipeline correction (W9.3) as a safety net for any remaining bad merges. With the global dendrogram already reducing wrong merges, fewer clusters will be flagged as suspicious, so the correction loop is cheap.
Together these address both failure modes: (a) greedily wrong merges that the global dendrogram prevents upfront, and (b) merges that looked correct from the noisy pre-ICM labels but were actually wrong, which the post-pipeline correction catches using the improved post-EM labels.
Hard Score merge candidates by ICM-energy change. For each pair of candidate sub-clusters, tentatively merge them, run a short ICM pass (5–10 sweeps), and record the change in total MRF energy. Accept the merge with the largest energy decrease; revert all others. This uses the spatial model itself as the merge oracle — a merge that creates territorial violations will increase energy, a correct merge will decrease it. Expensive (one ICM run per candidate pair per step) but fully principled and naturally handles any ramp variant.
Implementation
Reverted Average linkage: average linkage changes the greedy merge order, which changes which cells belong to each cluster mid-merge, which changes which pairs pass the spatial gates. Requires co-designing the gate structure with the linkage criterion.
Done Constrained global dendrogram implemented in _merge_clusters_to_k_global, enabled by default (use_global_merge=True). Spatial gates are evaluated once on the original sub-cluster cell sets before any merging — this is the key fix for the greedy average-linkage failure mode. A complete-linkage dendrogram is built over all valid pairs (invalid pairs get ∞ distance); fcluster cuts at K_target. Falls back to the greedy algorithm if scipy linkage fails or the dendrogram cannot be cut at exactly K_target clusters. Feature distances are capped at merge_max_cells_for_dist=200 cells per sub-cluster for scalability.
Default off Post-pipeline merge correction implemented (remerge_after_cleanup=False default). After the full cleanup, _split_fragmented_clusters re-detects spatial disconnectedness in the final K clusters; any cluster with ≥ 2 spatial components is re-split and the sub-clusters are re-merged using the global dendrogram on the post-pipeline (post-EM) labels, which are far better calibrated than the noisy pre-ICM labels used in the original merge. A final cleanup pass resolves post-correction violations.
W10 Coverage fraction conflates geometry and labelling — false positives at boundaries data

CF measures convex-hull polygon overlap, not actual territorial violation. Two cells from different types near a mosaic boundary can have low CF and be correctly labelled, while two same-type elongated cells at a tile edge can appear to overlap due to hull geometry even though their Voronoi cells don't conflict. The algorithm treats CF purely as a proxy for label error, but the CF distribution also encodes cell shape and mosaic regularity — confounds that are not disentangled.

Solutions
Moderate ★ Recommended Use actual Voronoi cell intersection instead of convex-hull CF. The Voronoi polygons (already computed in the dataset) tile space without overlap by construction. Switching _build_coverage_map to use actual polygon intersection area (via shapely) instead of convex-hull IoU would eliminate hull-induced false overlaps from elongated cells. Adds a shapely dependency but no algorithmic change to the rest of the pipeline.
Moderate Normalise CF by expected same-type baseline. For each cell pair, compute the expected CF under the null hypothesis that both cells are same-type (estimated from all same-label pairs' CF distribution). Use the standardised residual (CF − μ_same) / σ_same as the input to the ramp instead of raw CF. This removes the cell-shape confound and makes the ramp thresholds geometry-independent.
Hard Learn a CF → violation-probability mapping from labelled pairs. Train a logistic regression or isotonic regression mapping CF to P(label error) using the frozen cells (same/diff label) as training signal. Use the predicted probability directly as the pairwise weight instead of the ramp. This absorbs all geometric confounds into the model, but reintroduces the circularity of W2 at the pairwise-weight level.
Implementation
Already done coverage_fraction already passes the raw Voronoi polygon vertices to shapely's Polygon() directly — no convex-hull step exists. The "convex hull" language in the docstring was misleading. Voronoi cells are convex by construction, so convex hull = actual polygon. No code change needed.
W11 Only pairwise interactions are modelled — higher-order mosaic constraints are ignored energy

The MRF energy uses only pairwise cliques (cell pairs). The mosaic constraint is inherently a global, topological property (each type tiles the entire domain without same-type overlaps), which pairwise interactions only approximate locally. Higher-order cliques (e.g., penalising same-label connected components spanning multiple territories) could encode the constraint more accurately but are computationally intractable with ICM. This is a fundamental limitation of the pairwise MRF formulation.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended Add a global cell-count regulariser. Augment the unary with a per-label penalty β · |n_k − n_expected|², where n_expected = n_cells / K. This softly enforces equal cluster sizes (reasonable for balanced mosaics) without any higher-order clique computation. β can be calibrated similarly to lam.
Moderate Connected-component penalty post-ICM. After ICM converges, penalise same-label connected components that are larger than p× the expected tile size (estimated as bounding box area / K). Large same-label components that span multiple spatial territories are almost certainly labelling errors. Implement as a post-processing step that adds a per-component cost to the unary and runs one additional ICM pass.
Extreme Higher-order CRF with Pn Potts model. Replace the pairwise MRF with a higher-order CRF that includes clique potentials over all cells in a spatial neighbourhood (Pn model). Minimisation via α-expansion on the resulting higher-order energy is theoretically possible but requires specialised solvers and the complexity of the energy formulation grows rapidly with clique size.
Implementation
Done count_reg=0.0 parameter added. Applied as a fixed unary bias before ICM. No complications as opt-in.
W12 K-NN split detection is insensitive to cluster scale and density algorithm

_split_fragmented_clusters uses a fixed k_nn=5 k-NN graph built from spatial coordinates to detect disconnected components. For large or sparse clusters, 5 neighbours may not connect spatially extended but genuinely connected groups, causing spurious splits. For small clusters (n < 6), k_actual = min(k_nn+1, n) is used, but the connectivity threshold does not adapt to cluster-specific density. There is no sensitivity analysis or confidence measure associated with a detected split.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended Adaptive k scaled by cluster size. Replace the fixed k_nn=5 with k_nn = max(5, int(sqrt(n_k))). For a cluster of 100 cells, k=10 provides much denser connectivity and avoids spurious fragmentation of large but regularly-spaced clusters. A single-line change in _split_fragmented_clusters.
Moderate ★ Recommended Use DBSCAN instead of k-NN connectivity. Replace the k-NN graph with DBSCAN (eps = mean_nearest_neighbour_distance × 2, min_samples=2) to find disconnected spatial components within each cluster. DBSCAN naturally handles varying densities and irregular shapes, and its eps parameter can be set from the cluster's own nearest-neighbour distance distribution rather than a global constant.
Moderate Gap criterion on the minimum spanning tree. Build the Euclidean MST over each cluster's cells. A genuine spatial split corresponds to a large gap in the MST edge-length distribution (a bimodal distribution with a long-range edge separating the two components). Cutting at 2× the median edge length identifies real spatial gaps without sensitivity to k.
Implementation
Done Adaptive k (k_effective = max(k_nn, int(sqrt(n_k)))) implemented in _split_fragmented_clusters. No complications.
Default off DBSCAN implemented (use_dbscan_split=False default). Key insight: DBSCAN with adaptive eps = 2.5 × median_NN_dist can over-split clusters in some configs (e.g. "varied"), creating too many sub-clusters that the spatial-gated merge step cannot collapse back to K. Safe as opt-in once the merge robustness is improved.
W13 No global energy is tracked — convergence quality cannot be assessed convergence

The returned model dict tracks violations_before/after (count of same-label repulsive pairs) and n_changed (cells that changed label), but not the total MRF energy Σ unary[i, k] + lam Σ w_ij [k_i == k_j]. Without total energy, it is impossible to compare two runs (different seeds, configurations) on the same input, to verify that ICM did not increase energy (which should not happen but is useful to assert), or to detect premature convergence to a high-energy local minimum.

Solutions
Easy ★ Recommended Compute and store total MRF energy in the model dict. After each ICM pass, compute E = Σ_i unary[i, labels[i]] + lam × Σ_(i,j) w_ij × [labels[i] == labels[j]]. Store energy_before_icm, energy_after_icm, and energy_final in the returned model dict. The pairwise sum is already computed implicitly during ICM; factoring it out is a minor refactor. Enables multi-run comparison and sanity assertions.
Easy ★ Recommended Use energy delta as the ICM convergence criterion. Replace if n_changed_this_iter == 0: break with if ΔE < tol: break (relative energy change below a tolerance). This is strictly more principled: a sweep that makes many small flips at nearly equal energy should stop sooner, while a sweep with few but large-energy flips should continue. Requires energy tracking from the first solution above.
Implementation
Done Energy tracking: _compute_energy added. energy_before_icm, energy_after_icm, energy_final stored in model dict. Used by W1 multi-restart to select the best run. No complications.
Default off Energy delta convergence implemented (energy_tol=0.0 default). Key insight: stopping ICM early (even at relative ΔE < 10⁻⁴) leaves frozen cells with unresolved violations. In conflict resolution, a frozen cell with multiple same-label violating neighbours accumulates large pairwise neighbourhood energy, making its local minimum favour switching labels — a cascade that was absent when ICM ran to full convergence. Energy-delta stopping is safe only when paired with frozen-cell protection in conflict resolution.
Real-World Data

Adaptations for Real-World Data

6 items

Context

The algorithm was validated on toy mosaics: n ≤ 2 000 cells, K ≤ 6 types, balanced group sizes, complete coverage, and 2D–10D Gaussian features. Real-world retinal data differs in every dimension: high-dimensional features (D > 20), large datasets (n up to ~50 000 cells per retinal patch), systematic missing cells from exclusion zones or tissue damage, many types (K = 15+), wildly unbalanced group sizes (>20× ratio), and polygon scales that vary strongly across cell types (small midget vs. large parasol cells). Each card identifies which algorithmic components are most affected, what immediate parameter changes are needed, and what code-level changes would be required for full production robustness.

RWD1 High-dimensional features (D > 20) data energy

The GMM with covariance_type="full" has K·D(D+1)/2 free parameters. For D=50, K=15 this is ~19 000 parameters — likely more than the number of cells per cluster. Near-singular covariance matrices produce unstable log-likelihoods and meaningless unary costs. The calibrated lam also becomes inconsistent: the median unary gap scales with D (each dimension contributes a term in the Gaussian log-likelihood), inflating the gap without a matching increase in the pairwise penalty scale, so the same lam_alpha under-specifies lam. KDE unary is unreliable in high-D (all pairwise distances converge, making the bandwidth meaningless). The EM re-fit (n_em_iters=1, default) refits the same GMM architecture and carries the same overfitting risk.

Immediate parameter tuning (no code change)
Easy ★ Required Switch to covariance_type="diag". Reduces parameters from K·D²/2 to K·D, eliminates singular-covariance failures, and is still expressive when clusters differ along axis-aligned feature dimensions. For shared covariance structure, "tied" further reduces to D²/2 parameters total.
Easy ★ Recommended Pre-process with PCA to 10–20 components. Apply externally before creating the MosaicDataset. Removes collinear dimensions, brings full-covariance GMM into a tractable regime, and makes the unary gap scale independent of the original D.
Easy Increase lam_alpha to 3.0–5.0. Compensates for the larger raw unary gap at higher D. Monitor the calibrated lam printed in tau_calibration["lam_source"] to check whether the calibrated value is in a sensible range for the data scale.
Code changes needed for production
Medium Dimension-normalised lam calibration. Divide the median unary gap by D (or by the expected gap under a unit isotropic Gaussian in D dimensions) before computing the lam target. Makes lam_alpha dimensionally consistent across different feature spaces without manual tuning.
Hard Feature-transform parameter. Add a feature_transform argument (callable applied to X before GMM fitting and unary computation) so dimensionality reduction can be encapsulated inside the strategy rather than applied externally. Prevents the transform from being omitted accidentally at inference time.
RWD2 Large datasets (n > 10 000 cells) algorithm

The Gauss-Seidel ICM loop in _step_icm is a pure Python for i in range(n_cells) loop — O(n × mean_neighbors × K) per sweep. For n=50 000, K=15, mean_neighbors=10 this is 7.5 M Python operations per sweep. The Jacobi path (icm_jacobi=True) replaces this with a vectorised sparse matrix–vector multiply and a numpy argmin — orders of magnitude faster. The conflict-resolution inner loop is similarly O(n_conflicts × K²) in Python. The coverage-map build is O(n log n) via KD-tree search and is already parallelisable with n_workers. _compute_energy is O(n + edges) and is called every sweep when energy_tol > 0.

Immediate parameter tuning (no code change)
Easy ★ Required Use icm_jacobi=True. The vectorised Jacobi path is orders of magnitude faster than the Python GS loop for large n. Jacobi and GS may converge to slightly different local minima (W3); use n_restarts > 1 and compare by energy if reproducibility matters.
Easy ★ Recommended Use n_workers > 1. The coverage-map build is embarrassingly parallel; setting n_workers=8 on a machine with 8 cores reduces build time proportionally at no algorithmic cost.
Easy Keep energy_tol=0.0. With icm_jacobi=True, the n-changed criterion is free (just a sum over labels). Enabling energy_tol adds a Python-loop O(n + edges) energy computation each sweep — expensive for large n and only beneficial for early stopping when the Python GS path is used.
Code changes needed for production
Medium Auto-switch to Jacobi above an n threshold. Add an icm_jacobi="auto" option that selects Jacobi when n > 5 000, removing the need for manual selection. The Jacobi path is already fully implemented — only the dispatch logic needs changing.
Medium Vectorise conflict resolution. The K×K sub-problem in _resolve_conflicting_pairs is solved by a Python double-loop. Replacing it with numpy broadcasting — e_i[None,:] + e_j[:,None] + cross_term — eliminates the inner loop and makes the per-pair cost O(K²) vectorised rather than O(K²) interpreted.
RWD3 Many missing cells (non-uniform coverage, holes in territories) data algorithm

Systematic missing cells create holes inside cluster territories. _split_fragmented_clusters cannot distinguish a correct cluster with a hole in its interior from a genuine wrong merge — both appear as ≥ 2 disconnected spatial components. Spurious splits inflate K_eff and force the global dendrogram to merge many more sub-clusters, making the spatial gates more likely to fail. Tau calibration is affected because frozen same-label pairs near holes are sparse: fewer calibration points → more fallback to hard priors. The H1 pre-pass (theta_hard) may incorrectly unfreeze cells at hole boundaries where two same-type cells happen to face each other across the gap with high CF. Cells at the edge of a hole have fewer pairwise neighbors and thus lower total neighborhood energy — ICM may not correct their violations without help.

Immediate parameter tuning (no code change)
Easy ★ Recommended Keep use_dbscan_split=False (default). The k-NN path with adaptive k is more conservative than DBSCAN — it requires cells to be truly isolated before splitting. For missing-cell datasets, DBSCAN over-splits at hole boundaries where the local point density drops below its eps threshold.
Easy ★ Recommended Increase merge_min_adj_pairs to 3–5. Requires more cell-pair evidence before two sub-clusters are declared spatially adjacent. Prevents spurious merges across hole gaps where only 1–2 cell pairs span the void.
Easy Lower tau_low_q to 0.95. With fewer frozen same-label pairs (many cells missing), the p99 estimate is based on few points and is noisy. Using p95 gives a more stable upper bound of the same-label CF distribution.
Moderate Disable theta_hard (set to None, default). Near missing-cell holes, two cells of the same type facing each other across a gap may have high CF despite being correctly labelled — theta_hard would incorrectly unfreeze them and expose them to ICM pressure. Only enable theta_hard after manually verifying that violations in the data are genuine merge errors rather than hole-boundary artefacts.
Code changes needed for production
Hard Feature-bimodality guard on split detection. Before treating a detected spatial disconnection as a genuine split, test whether the two components differ in feature space (e.g. two-vs-one component GMM BIC test). Only split if both spatial AND feature evidence support a merge error. This eliminates spurious splits at hole boundaries where both components share the same feature distribution.
Medium Hole-aware calibration weighting. Down-weight CF calibration pairs that have a missing cell within radius r of either endpoint. These pairs are at risk of being same-type cells facing each other across a hole rather than genuine intra-tile contacts or cross-tile violations, and should not anchor tau_high estimation.
RWD4 Many groups (K = 15+) algorithm calibration

With K=15 types there are C(15,2)=105 cluster pairs. Most will have fewer than _MIN_CALIB_PAIRS=10 adjacent frozen-cell CF pairs → tau_high and lam calibration fall back to hard priors for the majority of pairs. The per-cluster tau (default on) is calibrated from frozen same-label pairs within each cluster, but small clusters may still lack sufficient frozen pairs. The split-merge pipeline with n_clusters_init = 2×K = 30 sub-clusters requires the global dendrogram to cut at 15 — the constraint that every sub-cluster pair within a target merged group must be spatially valid can fail for asymmetric mosaic geometries, forcing a greedy fallback. GMM initialisation with k-means is increasingly unreliable at K=15+ in high-dimensional space.

Immediate parameter tuning (no code change)
Easy ★ Recommended Switch to init="leiden" with leiden_k_features=20–30. Leiden builds a feature-space graph and finds communities — more robust than k-means for high K and high D. Set leiden_resolution=None to auto-calibrate for the target K.
Easy ★ Recommended Increase n_clusters_init to 3×K. For K=15, use n_clusters_init=45. More sub-clusters give the merge step more flexibility to find the globally optimal partition. The global dendrogram overhead is O(K_eff³) ≈ 90 000 operations for K_eff=45 — negligible.
Easy ★ Recommended Enable cf_tau_mixture=True. The two-component CF GMM estimates tau_low without requiring labeled frozen pairs. For K=15, the within-type CF population is still distinguishable from the cross-type population even when per-pair frozen data is sparse.
Moderate Enable recalibrate=True. After the main ICM, labels are better than the raw GMM. A second calibration pass on the post-ICM labels finds more frozen pairs with more accurate labels, improving tau_high and lam estimates. Cost: one extra calibration + ICM pass.
Code changes needed for production
Easy Expose _MIN_CALIB_PAIRS as a parameter. Currently a module-level constant (10). For K=15 with sparse pairs, a lower threshold (5–8) may be needed to avoid excessive fallback to priors. Alternatively, auto-scale as max(5, n_cells / (K² × mean_neighbors)).
Hard Per-cluster-pair lam calibration. Analogous to per_cluster_tau, calibrate lam independently per cluster pair from the frozen pairs between those two clusters. A pair of very similar types (small unary gap) needs a lower lam than a pair of very different types. Requires storing a K×K lam matrix and looking up the appropriate value during ICM.
RWD5 Highly imbalanced group sizes (>20× ratio) algorithm calibration

With cluster sizes ranging from, say, 10 to 200+ cells, several components break down. The global median_push and median_unary_gap used for lam calibration are dominated by large clusters — lam is calibrated to flip cells in large clusters, which may be far too weak to correct boundary errors in small clusters that face constant neighborhood pressure from surrounding large clusters. Small clusters may have no frozen cells at all (too few cells to reliably reach conf_threshold), preventing per-cluster tau calibration from operating and leaving them unprotected. GMM k-means initialisation tends toward equal-size regions in feature space — small clusters may be initialised inside a large cluster's territory. The count regulariser (count_reg > 0) must remain off (default 0.0): the equal-size prior penalises the correct solution.

Immediate parameter tuning (no code change)
Easy ★ Required Keep count_reg=0.0 (default). The equal-size prior actively harms imbalanced data — never enable it for RWD with unequal group sizes.
Easy ★ Recommended Switch to init="agglomerative". Ward-linkage agglomerative clustering tends to produce more size-proportional initial clusters than k-means, because it merges based on within-cluster variance rather than distance to a centroid.
Easy ★ Recommended Increase conf_threshold to 0.95–0.99. Small clusters are typically feature-coherent; raising the threshold ensures only the most confident small-cluster cells are frozen. This is especially important because even one incorrectly-frozen cell in a 10-cell cluster occupies 10 % of its population.
Moderate Increase n_restarts to 3–5. For highly imbalanced data, GMM initialisation is more likely to produce degenerate starting points (a small cluster absorbed into a large one). Multi-restart with best-energy selection provides a safety net without algorithmic changes.
Code changes needed for production
Hard Per-cluster lam calibration (see RWD4). For imbalanced data this is especially critical: a cell in a 10-cell cluster needs proportionally higher lam to resist the majority's neighbourhood pressure than a cell in a 200-cell cluster.
Medium Size-weighted GMM initialisation. When prior knowledge of cluster size ratios is available (e.g. from literature cell-type counts), provide weights_init to the initial GMM. Add a cluster_size_prior: list[float] | None = None parameter that, when set, overrides the uniform weight initialisation.
RWD6 Heterogeneous polygon sizes across cell types data calibration

When cell types differ strongly in polygon area, several components require attention. A single fixed spatial_radius may be too small for large-polygon cells to find any neighbors in the coverage map — their adjacent cells' centroids may lie farther away than the radius — while being too large for small cells (pulling in non-adjacent far-away cells). The global tau calibration is then dominated by the majority (medium-size) type: large-polygon cells naturally generate higher intra-tile CF values even in a correct mosaic, so the global tau_low will be too low for them (triggering false violations). The signed ramp's attractive zone (CF < tau_low) may never fire for large cells whose same-tile CF is always above the global tau_low. The per-cluster tau (S12, default on) partially addresses this, but only when frozen same-label pairs are available for that cluster.

Immediate parameter tuning (no code change)
Easy ★ Required Set spatial_radius to the diameter of the largest cell type. Estimate as 2 × sqrt(max_polygon_area / π). If the radius is too small for large-polygon cells, their genuine same-label violating pairs are not in raw_map at all — ICM cannot correct those violations regardless of lam.
Easy ★ Required Leave per_cluster_tau=True (default). This is the most important existing safeguard: each cluster calibrates its tau_low from its own same-label CFs, automatically giving large-polygon clusters a higher dead zone.
Easy ★ Recommended Enable polygon_dilation=0.05–0.1. Dilation is a fraction of each cell's own diameter — scale-invariant by design. For large cells, this ensures that touching same-tile pairs (CF ≈ 0 without dilation) register a small non-zero CF, giving the signed ramp an attractive signal for genuine intra-tile contacts.
Moderate Enable cf_tau_mixture=True. The two-component CF GMM separates the full CF distribution into a low component (intra-tile contacts) and a high component (violations) without relying on labels. For heterogeneous polygon sizes, these two populations are typically still distinguishable and the mixture model is more robust than the label-based quantile for setting a single tau_low.
Code changes needed for production
Hard Cell-adaptive spatial radius. Replace the single global spatial_radius with a per-cell radius proportional to each cell's polygon diameter (e.g. r_i = 2 × sqrt(area_i / π)). The coverage-map builder would search within max(r_i, r_j) for each pair (i, j). This ensures that all geometrically adjacent pairs are captured regardless of polygon size disparity.
Medium Area-normalised CF variant. Replace the current intersection/union CF with intersection / min(A_i, A_j). Under this normalisation a small cell whose polygon is entirely inside a large cell's polygon gets CF=1 (a severe violation), while the inverse (large cell barely touching a small cell) gives a CF proportional to the fraction of the small cell covered. More sensitive to genuine violations for mixed-size pairs, at the cost of asymmetry.
